Black Pagoda & Sun God's Chariot

Konark Sun Temple, built in 1250 CE by King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga Dynasty, is an architectural masterpiece representing the Sun God Surya's colossal stone chariot. The temple showcases Kalinga architecture at its peak, with intricate stone carvings depicting mythology, astronomy, and daily life.

The temple is designed as a gigantic chariot with 24 elaborately carved stone wheels (representing hours) pulled by seven horses (representing days of the week). Each architectural element has astronomical significance.

Known as the "Black Pagoda" to European sailors, the temple once served as a navigation landmark. The main sanctum collapsed centuries ago, but the remaining structures continue to awe visitors with their scale and artistry.

The temple complex demonstrates advanced engineering: the wheels function as sundials, the main tower aligned to capture the sun's first rays, and magnet placement techniques that reportedly made the main idol float in air.

Declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1984, Konark represents "the highest point of achievement of Kalinga architecture" with its harmonious integration of architectural form with sculptural ornamentation.
